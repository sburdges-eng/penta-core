## I. Psychoacoustic & Biological Triggers (The Visceral)

These exploit the human ear's hardware and evolutionary survival instincts.

### 1. Infrasound (<20Hz)
Frequencies below human hearing that cause physical unease and nausea. Used extensively in horror films (Paranormal Activity, Irreversible). The body feels what the ears cannot consciously hear.

### 2. The Shepard Tone
An auditory illusion of a pitch that continually ascends or descends but never actually gets higher or lower. Creates infinite anxiety and tension. Famously used in The Dark Knight (Dunkirk used a visual-audio version with the ticking watch).

### 3. Non-Linear Sounds
Mimicking the acoustic structure of a scream (chaos, distortion, unpredictable frequency spikes) in instruments to trigger fight-or-flight responses. Research shows the brain processes these similarly to actual screams.

### 4. Heartbeat Entrainment
Setting the BPM to approximately:
- **~60 BPM**: Resting heart rate, induces calm
- **~120 BPM**: Elevated/stress heart rate, induces panic
- **~180+ BPM**: Extreme exertion, creates unsustainable tension

### 5. Binaural Beats
Slight frequency differences between left and right channels to induce specific brainwave states:
- **Delta (0.5-4Hz)**: Deep sleep, unconscious
- **Theta (4-8Hz)**: Drowsy, meditative
- **Alpha (8-14Hz)**: Relaxed, calm awareness
- **Beta (14-30Hz)**: Alert, focused
- **Gamma (30-100Hz)**: Peak concentration, anxiety

### 6. The "Gasp" Breath
Adding a sharp intake of breath before a drop or transition unconsciously stops the listener's breathing. Creates physiological anticipation.

### 7. High-Frequency "Tinnitus" Ringing
Using a sine wave >12kHz to simulate the physiological sound of shock or blast trauma. Saving Private Ryan uses this extensively during the Omaha Beach sequence.

### 8. Sub-Bass Throbs
Rhythmic pulses at 40Hz to rattle the listener's chest cavity during "safe" scenes. Suggests unseen danger without explicit musical signaling.

### 9. Biological Mimicry
Using instruments that sound like:
- Bones cracking
- Fluids rushing/blood pumping
- Flies buzzing
- Predator growls
- Snake hisses
- Heartbeats

### 10. The "Startle" Reflex
Sudden, loud transients (stingers) after prolonged silence. The classic jumpscare audio component exploits the acoustic startle reflex.

### 11. Fletcher-Munson Manipulation
Boosting 3-4kHz (where the human ear is most sensitive—the frequency range of baby cries and screams) to make a sound piercing and urgent.

### 12. Stereo Width Collapse
Slowly narrowing the stereo image to mono to create feelings of:
- Claustrophobia
- Entrapment
- Focus/tunnel vision
- Isolation

### 13. Stereo Width Expansion
Sudden widening to side channels to represent:
- Freedom
- The sublime
- Revelation
- Transcendence

### 14. Dry Vocals
Removing all reverb to make a voice sound like it is inside the listener's head. Creates uncomfortable intimacy and paranoia.

### 15. Hyper-Real Foley
Amplifying tiny sounds (cloth rustle, swallows, breathing, heartbeats) to create uncomfortable intimacy or heightened awareness.

## II. Harmonic Subversion (The Intellectual)

Techniques that exploit learned musical associations and expectations.

### 16. Tritone (The Devil's Interval)
The diminished fifth, historically banned by the church. Creates unresolved tension and evil associations.

### 17. Minor Second Clusters
Stacking notes one semitone apart creates extreme dissonance. Used for psychological horror and madness.

### 18. Deceptive Cadences
Setting up an expected resolution (V-I) but delivering something else (V-vi). Creates narrative misdirection.

### 19. Lydian Mode for Wonder
The raised 4th creates a sense of floating, wonder, and magic (E.T., Up, Avatar).

### 20. Phrygian Mode for Threat
The lowered 2nd creates exotic threat and impending doom (Middle Eastern villain clichés, but also effective).

### 21. Locrian Mode for Instability
The diminished tonic makes nothing feel grounded. Pure instability.

### 22. Polytonality
Two keys simultaneously creates cognitive dissonance and "wrongness" without explicit dissonance.

### 23. Modal Mixture
Borrowing chords from parallel modes (major to minor and vice versa) for emotional complexity.

### 24. Plagal Cadence for Transcendence
IV-I (the "Amen" cadence) for religious, spiritual, or death scenes.

### 25. Unresolved Dominant
Leaving a V chord hanging creates unbearable tension demanding resolution.

### 26. The Picardy Third
Ending a minor piece on a major chord. Creates bittersweet resolution or false hope.

### 27. Chromatic Descent (Lamento Bass)
Descending chromatic bass line associated with grief since the Baroque era.

### 28. Ascending Chromatic Lines
Suggests building threat or approaching danger.

### 29. Quartal Harmony
Stacked 4ths instead of 3rds. Ambiguous, neither major nor minor. Used for neutral/alien soundscapes.

### 30. Whole Tone Scale
Every note is a whole step apart. Creates dreamlike, floating, disorienting quality (Debussy, dream sequences).

## III. Temporal Manipulation (Time Perception)

### 31. Metric Modulation
Smoothly changing tempo by reinterpreting subdivisions. Disorients the listener's sense of time.

### 32. Tempo Rubato
Subtle speeding up and slowing down to breathe life into mechanical-seeming music.

### 33. Polyrhythm
Multiple rhythmic patterns simultaneously (3 against 4, 5 against 7). Creates complexity and unease.

### 34. Isorhythm
A repeating rhythmic pattern with a non-aligning melodic pattern. Creates slow revelation.

### 35. Accelerando into Nothing
Building tempo to breaking point then cutting to silence.

### 36. Ritardando for Death
Slowing tempo to suggest life draining, time stopping.

### 37. Syncopation Disruption
Breaking expected rhythmic patterns to create anxiety.

### 38. Ostinato Hypnosis
Repeating patterns that induce trance-like states.

### 39. Time Signature Obscuration
Using uncommon or changing time signatures (7/8, 5/4, shifting) to disorient.

### 40. Silence as Tempo
Using the absence of sound to reset the listener's temporal expectations.

## IV. Spectral & Timbral Design

### 41. Prepared Piano
Objects placed on strings for unexpected timbres (horror, avant-garde).

### 42. Bowed Metal
Cymbals, vibraphone, or metal sheets bowed create unsettling sustained tones.

### 43. Granular Synthesis
Breaking sounds into tiny grains and reassembling. Creates otherworldly textures.

### 44. Spectral Freezing
Holding a single moment of a sound's spectrum indefinitely.

### 45. Ring Modulation
Creates inharmonic, metallic, robotic timbres. Classic for alien/robot voices.

### 46. Vocoder Processing
Imposing one sound's spectrum on another. Human/machine hybrid sounds.

### 47. Harmonic Distortion vs. Intermodulation
Different types of distortion for different emotional qualities.

### 48. Formant Shifting
Changing vocal character without changing pitch. Uncanny valley effects.

### 49. Convolution with Unusual Spaces
Reverbing with impulse responses of coffins, skulls, caves, etc.

### 50. Spectral Blur
Smearing frequency content across time for ghostly, ethereal qualities.

## V. Spatial Audio Techniques

### 51. Sound Source Movement
Moving sound around the listener for disorientation or focus.

### 52. Height Channels (Atmos/Auro)
Using overhead speakers for transcendence, falling, or surveillance.

### 53. LFE Isolation
Putting only sub-bass in the .1 channel for physical impact without masking.

### 54. Discrete vs. Diffuse Placement
Point sources for focus; diffuse for atmosphere.

### 55. Near-Field Intimacy
Sounds that appear to originate from within the listener's personal space.

### 56. Far-Field Isolation
Sounds that appear distant to create loneliness or vastness.

### 57. Acoustic Environment Shifts
Changing reverb to suggest location/psychological state changes.

### 58. Head-Locked vs. World-Locked
Whether sound follows camera or stays in scene space.

### 59. Phantom Center Manipulation
Using hard panning to make the center disappear.

### 60. Rear Channel Threat
Placing danger behind the listener.

## VI. Dynamic & Structural Techniques

### 61. Extreme Dynamic Range
Forcing listeners to turn up volume during quiet parts, making loud parts devastating.

### 62. Compression for Relentlessness
Limiting dynamic range to create exhausting, unrelenting intensity.

### 63. The Drop Out
Sudden removal of all low end or all high end.

### 64. The False Ending
Music that appears to resolve but continues.

### 65. Anticlimax Cadence
Building up then deflating without payoff.

### 66. Motivic Development
Transforming themes to reflect character arcs.

### 67. Leitmotif Corruption
Gradually distorting a recognizable theme.

### 68. Stinger to Ostinato
Transitioning from one-shot impacts to repeating patterns.

### 69. Cross-Fade Bleed
Letting scenes' music bleed into each other for continuity.

### 70. Diegetic/Non-Diegetic Blurring
Music that exists both in the film world and as score.

### 72. The Romantic Tristan Chord
Wagner's unresolved longing chord.

### 73. The Theremin
Historic association with aliens and madness.

### 74. Glass Armonica
High-frequency, ethereal tones associated with ghosts.

### 75. Children's Voices
Innocence, or corrupted innocence in horror contexts.

### 76. Music Box
Childhood, memory, or sinister nursery.

### 77. Church Bells
Announcements, death, religion, time.

### 78. Ethnic Instruments for "Other"
Problematic but common: exotic instruments for foreign settings.

### 79. Military Snare
Duty, discipline, war.

### 80. Solo Piano for Intimacy

## VIII. Production Techniques

### 81. Sidechain Pumping
Ducking music under dialogue rhythmically.

### 82. Multiband Ducking
Surgical frequency-specific ducking.

### 83. Parallel Compression
Adding compressed signal to uncompressed for power + dynamics.

### 84. Saturation Warmth
Subtle harmonic distortion for analog feel.

### 85. High-Pass Telephone Effect
Filtering for memory/flashback/communication.

### 86. Low-Pass Underwater Effect
Muffled for submersion or isolation.

### 87. Reverse Reverb
Pre-echoes before sounds for supernatural anticipation.

### 88. Gated Reverb
80s signature but useful for powerful, controlled space.

### 89. Phasing/Flanging
Time-based effects for psychedelic or dreamy states.

### 90. Bit Crushing
Digital destruction for retro or broken technology.

## Ethical Considerations

While these techniques are powerful creative tools, be mindful of:

1. **Photosensitive/Auditory Epilepsy**: Extreme frequency content can trigger seizures
2. **PTSD Triggers**: Realistic trauma sounds may affect vulnerable audiences
3. **Hearing Damage**: Extended high-SPL content, especially in low frequencies
4. **Manipulation Ethics**: These techniques bypass conscious processing—use responsibly
5. **Cultural Sensitivity**: Avoid stereotypical ethnic instrument associations
